2-D Tight Binding - Graphene¶

Graphene is a compound composed of a single layer of carbon atoms arranged in a hexagonal lattice. It posesses some interesting properties that make it a possible candidate for the next generation of semiconductors. Solid state physicists use a quantum mechanical approximation called the Tight Binding Model to calculate these properties. The model assumes that electrons are generally tightly bound to their atoms, but they may hop to different atoms at a certain energy cost. This simple model leads to complex behavior.

Graphene's "hexagonal" lattice can properly be thought of as a diamond-shaped unit cell containing two atoms A and B. If $a$ represents the distance between carbon atoms, then the unit cell can be defined by two basis vectors, $\vec{a_1}=\frac{a}{2}(3,\sqrt{3})$ and $\vec{a_2}=\frac{a}{2}(3,-\sqrt{3})$.

To examine the electron energy spectrum, it's much more useful to look at this problem in momentum space. This involves a Fourier Transform from the original position vectors $\vec{a_1}$ and $\vec{a_2}$ to the momentum vectors $\vec{b_1}$ and $\vec{b_2}$. The unit cell in momentum space is known as a Brillouin Zone. The points $\Gamma$, K, and M represent points of reference that we use to display band structure.

Tight Binding Model¶

Since Graphene's unit cell has two atoms, the simplest possible tight binding model is represented by the hamiltonian

\begin{equation} H = -t\sum_i (A^\dagger_i B_i + B^\dagger_i A_i). + V_0\sum_i(A^\dagger_i A_i + B^\dagger B), \end{equation}

where $A$ and $B$ represent each atomic site, $t$ is the hopping energy, and $V_0$ is the binding energy. We can think of the hamiltonian as a matrix:

\begin{equation} H = \sum_i \begin{pmatrix} A_i^* \\ B_i^* \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} V_0 & -t \\ -t & V_0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} A_i & B_i \end{pmatrix}, \end{equation}

where $\mathcal{H}=\begin{pmatrix} V_0 & -t \\ -t & V_0 \end{pmatrix}$ is the kernel of the hamiltonian.

To compute what we're after (the electron energy spectrum), we'll want to convert this problem to momentum space. A Fourier Transform adds a factor of $e^{i \overrightarrow{k}\cdot\overrightarrow{r}}$ to each interaction, where $\vec{r}$ is the distance between the two particles in real space. The geometry of the problem gives us the three nearest-neighbor vectors,

\begin{align} \delta_1 &= \frac{a}{2}(1, \sqrt{3}) \\ \delta_2 &= \frac{a}{2}(1, -\sqrt{3}) \\ \delta_3 &= a(-1, 0). \end{align}

The hamiltonian matrix in momentum space is then

\begin{equation} \mathcal{H}(k) =\begin{pmatrix} V_0 & -t(e^{i \overrightarrow{k} \cdot \overrightarrow{\delta_1}} + e^{i \overrightarrow{k} \cdot \overrightarrow{\delta_2}} + e^{i \overrightarrow{k} \cdot \overrightarrow{\delta_3}}) \\ -t(e^{-i \overrightarrow{k} \cdot \overrightarrow{\delta_1}} + e^{-i \overrightarrow{k} \cdot \overrightarrow{\delta_2}} + e^{-i \overrightarrow{k} \cdot \overrightarrow{\delta_3}}) & V_0\end{pmatrix} \end{equation}

.

From here, computing the eigenvalues of the matrix will give us the energy spectrum we are looking for. This is a simple matter in NumPy:

In [3]:
v_0 = 0
t = 1
a = 1

d_1 = a * np.array((-1, 0))
d_2 = a/2 * np.array((1, np.sqrt(3)))
d_3 = a/2 * np.array((1, -np.sqrt(3)))

def hop(k, r):
    return -t * np.exp(1j * np.dot(k, r))
    
def hop_conj(k, r):
    return -t * np.exp(-1j * np.dot(k, r))

def energy(k):
    h = np.matrix([[v_0,  hop(k, d_1) + hop(k, d_2) + hop(k, d_3)],
                   [hop_conj(k, d_1) + hop_conj(k, d_2) + hop_conj(k, d_3), v_0]])
    return np.real(np.linalg.eigvals(h))

Let's get plotting! Typically we calculate the energy for a range of k-values, then plot it as a 2-dimensional slice of the system. Starting at point $\Gamma$ (the edge of the Brillouin Zone), sweep the K-values in a line to point K.

In [16]:
k = [(kx, kx/np.sqrt(3)) for kx in np.linspace(0, b, 100)]
bands = pd.DataFrame(list(map(energy, k))).apply(np.sort, axis=1)
bands.plot(title=r'Graphene Energy Bands ($\Gamma$K)', legend=());

As expected from the two atoms in each unit cell, there are two electron energy bands. This plot shows why K was chosen as a significant point: the energy bands meet here in a cone, allowing electrons to flow from one band to another with no energy cost.

2-d plots feel a bit limiting, don't they? Instead of calculating k-values in a line, we can compute the energy bands for the entire Brillouin Zone and use Matplotlib's 3d functions to get a better picture of the system.

from mpl_toolkits.mplot3d import Axes3D
fig = plt.figure()
axis = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
axis.set_title("Energy Bandstructure of Graphene")

kx = np.linspace(-2*b, 2*b, 100)
ky = np.linspace(-2*b/np.sqrt(3), 2*b/np.sqrt(3), 100)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(kx, ky)

Z = np.array(list(map(energy, zip(X.flatten(), Y.flatten()))))
axis.plot_surface(X, Y, Z[:,0].reshape((100,100)))
axis.plot_surface(X, Y, Z[:,1].reshape((100,100)));

With a 3-d plot, the hexagonal structure of the energy bands is visible, as are the cones at the K-points.
